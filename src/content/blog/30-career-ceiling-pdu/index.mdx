I am a self-described failed engineer.

That is not false modesty. I came from manufacturing and engineering, and at some point I realized the engineering life was not for me. What I found instead was project management, and it clicked in a way nothing else had. I have spent the years since building a career around it, stacking certifications, earning an MBA, and moving across domains to broaden my range.

And I am here to tell you that none of that, on its own, broke the ceiling.

---

## The Ceiling Is Real

There is a point in many PM careers where upward movement stalls. Not because of a lack of skill. Not because of a lack of education. Not because of a lack of ambition. It stalls because the market reads your profile and puts you in a box.

I have lived this. I started my PM career in a technical, acquisitional role. I moved laterally into the Agile side of the house to gain a deeper understanding of how delivery actually works at scale. I picked up the RTE role within SAFe. Every one of those moves made me a better practitioner. Every one of them broadened what I could do.

And every one of them made it harder to get back to a PM role.

That is the ceiling nobody talks about. It is not a skills gap. It is a typecasting problem.

---

## **Lateral Moves Feel Like Growth. They Can Also Be Traps.**

When you move from traditional PM into Agile, it feels like progress. When you step into an RTE role, it feels like a promotion. You are operating at the program level. You are facilitating PI Planning. You are coaching teams across an entire Agile Release Train. The scope is bigger. The complexity is real.

But the market does not always see it that way. What the market sees is your most recent title. And if your most recent title is RTE, then every recruiter, every hiring manager, every job algorithm slots you into the same lane: RTE, Senior Scrum Master, Agile Coach. The PM roles you are qualified for stop surfacing. The ones that do surface come with caveats.

I have seen this firsthand. I was offered a PM role. It was later retracted and restructured as a Senior Scrum Master position with PM responsibilities "by name." Same work. Different title. Different trajectory. That is not a career path. That is a lateral treadmill.

---

## **Credentials Open Doors. They Don't Choose Which Ones.**

I hold a PMP. I hold a SAFe 6 SPC. I have an MBA. On paper, that profile should open any PM door in the building.

In practice, credentials get filtered through whatever lens the hiring organization is using. If they see SAFe certs, they route you to SAFe roles. If they see RTE experience, they route you to RTE roles. The PMP becomes background noise because the Agile keywords are louder.

This is not a knock on certifications. They matter. They demonstrate commitment, knowledge, and professional discipline. But they do not override the typecasting problem. They are necessary, not sufficient.

The uncomfortable truth is that the more specialized your experience becomes, the more the market narrows its view of what you can do. Your breadth becomes invisible because the algorithms and the recruiters are optimizing for keyword matches, not career arcs.

---

## **The Typecasting Problem Is Structural, Not Personal.**

This is worth saying clearly: the ceiling is not your fault.

Organizations structure roles around frameworks, not around people. If a company runs SAFe, they hire RTEs. If a company runs Scrum, they hire Scrum Masters. If a company runs traditional delivery, they hire PMs. Very few organizations think in terms of "this person understands delivery at multiple levels and can operate in any of these models." They think in terms of role titles and framework alignment.

That structural reality means your career path is shaped as much by how organizations categorize work as by what you are actually capable of doing. And when you have spent time in multiple models, you end up being "too Agile" for traditional PM roles and "too PM" for pure Agile coaching roles. You fall into a gap that nobody designed a job description for.

---

## **Breaking the Ceiling Requires a Deliberate Move.**

Lateral moves alone will not do it. More certifications alone will not do it. What breaks the ceiling is a deliberate, strategic investment in something that changes how the market reads your profile.

For me, that means going back to school. I am starting an MSIT with a data analytics focus in June. That is not a casual decision. It is a calculated move to add a technical dimension that repositions my profile beyond the Agile/SAFe lane. Data analytics sits at the intersection of business intelligence, technology strategy, and delivery. It gives me a credential and a skillset that cannot be slotted into the RTE box.

That is my approach. Yours might be different. The point is not the specific path. The point is that breaking through requires you to actively disrupt how the market categorizes you. You have to give them a reason to read your profile differently.

---

## **Your Career Is Not a Straight Line. Stop Expecting It to Be.**

I came from manufacturing. I was an engineer who realized engineering was not the right fit. I moved into project management. I moved from technical PM work into Agile. I moved into the RTE role. I stacked an MBA, a PMP, and a SAFe SPC along the way. None of those transitions were part of some master plan I drew up on day one.

And that is fine. Careers are not waterfalls. They do not flow in one direction through neatly defined phases. They are iterative. They involve pivots, experiments, and course corrections. The "failed engineer" path led me to project management. The lateral moves into Agile and SAFe gave me a breadth of delivery experience that most PMs do not have. The ceiling I am facing now is pushing me to add data analytics to the mix.

Every one of those moves, including the ones that felt like setbacks, built something. The key is recognizing when a move has stopped building and started boxing you in. That is when you need to act.

---

## Final Thought

I am still very enthusiastic about my career path. That might sound contradictory after everything I just laid out about ceilings and typecasting, but it is not.

The ceiling is real. The typecasting is real. But so is the fact that I have built a career that spans manufacturing, engineering, technical program management, Agile delivery, and SAFe at scale. I am adding data analytics next. I am writing about what I have learned. I am building something.

The PMs who break through the ceiling are not the ones who wait for the right job posting to appear. They are the ones who refuse to let the market define them. They stack skills deliberately. They build in public. They stay uncomfortable on purpose.

If you are hitting the ceiling right now, know this: the ceiling is not the end of the story. It is the part of the story where you decide what comes next.
